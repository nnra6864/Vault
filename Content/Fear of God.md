Meaning of the fear of [[God]] has been heavily misunderstood and abused in the modern world.
By viewing God as a judge that waits for you to fall, and [[repentance]] as a transaction to pay off those [[sin]]s, literal fear of God becomes a necessity to avoid damnation.
[[Eastern Orthodoxy]] views this completely differently.
God is our heavenly father who offers us healing, and wants to see us grow in his love.
He is not just a judge, he is also a physician of the soul. His desire is our healing, restoration and communion.
Truly understanding this requires understanding the eastern orthodox view of [[Theosis]], which provides a much deeper framework for understanding [[Salvation]] and spiritual growth, and ties deeply into this topic.
With this in mind, there are 2 types of fear recognized in eastern orthodoxy.

## Servile Fear

Servile fear, or the fear of a slave, should only be the beginning of our spiritual lives.
It's a useful tool that stops us from committing horrible acts, but it's far away from the goal of salvation.
It's the last resort used to preserve humanity.
It is the literal fear of eternal torture stopping us from doing something extremely bad.
Sadly, this exact type of fear has always been abused.

## Filial Fear

Filial fear, or the fear of a son, is how we should ultimately view and understand the fear of God.
It is no longer primarily a fear of consequence, but rather a fear of disappointing God, who loves us infinitely and wants what's best for us, and a fear of being separated from the One we love.
It's the fear of disappointing someone who's so beyond our understanding, yet loves us perfectly and wants a relationship with us.
This doesn't orient our spiritual life around avoiding punishment, but around growing in communion with God in His love and energies, leading to true theosis.
This kind of love and fear of disappointment may be viewed as a form of [[χαρμολύπη]] ([[charmolypi]], sorrowful joy).
As we grow closer to God, the filial fear grows into awe and wonder before the infinite God, together with a deeper awareness of our smallness and dependence on Him.

## Evidence

- 1 John 4:18:
	There is no fear in love; but perfect love casts out fear, because fear involves torment. But he who fears has not been made perfect in love.
	> [!NOTE]
	> This clearly shows that the ultimate goal is not fear but being made perfect in love.

- St. Anthony the Great:
	I no longer fear God, but I love Him. For perfect love casts out fear.
	> [!NOTE]
	> This further strengthens the point made in 1 John 4:18.

- St. Dorotheos of Gaza:
	There are two kinds of fear: one introductory and the other perfect... The first is proper to beginners, while the second belongs to the saints who have reached perfect love. The person who does God's will out of fear of punishment is like a slave... but the one who fulfills it out of love is like a son. Through the introductory fear, we come to the perfect fear, which is the awe of standing before the majesty of the Beloved.
	> [!NOTE]
	> St. Dorotheos summarizes this entire note flawlessly.
	> He shows that what starts out as the fear of a slave, eventually grows into the fear of a son, and lastly into the perfect fear, a non tormenting awe of God.

- Romans 8:15:
	For you did not receive the spirit of bondage again to fear, but you received the Spirit of adoption by whom we cry, 'Abba, Father.'
	> [!NOTE]
	> This verse contrasts the spirit of bondage (living as a slave terrified of the master) with the Christian life.
	> Instead, we are like adopted children, not afraid of our father looking for an excuse to destroy us, but rather fearing anything that might damage the family relationship.

- Psalm 139:8 (LXX 138:8):
	If I ascend into [[heaven]], You are there; If I make my bed in [[hell]], behold, You are there.
	> [!NOTE]
	> Orthodox theologians often point to this verse to show that no creature can exist outside of God's presence.
	> This means that heaven and hell are not primarily a matter of location, but rather how His presence is experienced.

- Hebrews 12:29 & Deuteronomy 4:24:
	For our God is a consuming fire.
	> [!NOTE]
	> Orthodox theologians commonly interpret this passage to mean that God's presence is experienced as joy by the righteous, but torment by the fallen.
	> To a loving soul, this fire feels like warmth, love and theosis.
	> To a fallen soul, this fire feels like burning.
	
- St. Isaac the Syrian:
	I say that those who are punished in Gehenna (hell) are scourged by the **scourge of love**. For what is so bitter and vehement as the torment of love? I mean that those who become conscious that they have sinned against love suffer greater torment from this than from any fear of punishment.
	The sorrow caused in the heart by **sin against love** is more poignant than any torment... **It is totally wrong to think that the sinners in Gehenna are deprived of God's love.** ... But **the power of love works in two ways**: it torments those who have played the fool, even as happens here when a friend suffers from a friend; but it becomes a source of joy for those who have observed its duties.
	> [!NOTE]
	> St. Isaac is arguably one of the most famous examples for holding to this theology.
	> Note above perfectly aligns with the eastern view of theosis, as well as explaining the fear of God.
	
- St. Maximos the Confessor:
	He who fears the Lord has always a companion—humility... But **he who has progressed in love does not fear, but is filled with awe**. He does not fear punishment, but remembers the honor due to his Father. He is afraid of falling away from the grace given to him, because he knows how sweet it is to be in communion with God.
	> [!NOTE]
	> This is pretty much a perfect explanation of the fear of God
	
- St. John Chrysostom:
	To be separated from God is a greater punishment than hell itself. It is like a son who loves his father, for whom **the greatest punishment is not the rod, but seeing his father's face turned away**.
	> [!NOTE]
	> This yet again shows that our relationship with God shouldn't be the one of a slave and master, but rather a son and father.
	
- St. Silouan the Athonite:
	The Lord loves us so much that no one can describe it... **The soul that knows the love of God does not fear anything, but only grieves that she might offend her beloved Lord.** She knows that the Lord is merciful, and that He forgives sins, but she cannot bear to bring grief to Him who loves her so much.
	> [!NOTE]
	> We can see a clear continuation of this exact teaching well into the 20th century.
	> It is extremely consistent, from the very beginning of the Christianity, all the way to the present day.
	> This exceptional consistency shows how deeply rooted and fundamental this view of fear of God really is.
